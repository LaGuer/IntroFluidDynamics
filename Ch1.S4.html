1.4 Total Derivative and Acceleration

Now that we know how to visualize the flow of a fluid, we need to be able to calculate how it changes.

Let f⁢(x,y,z,t) be some quantity of interest – it might be a component of velocity (u or v, say), or maybe the density of the fluid ρ, or even the concentration c of a certain pollutant in the fluid. If we’re then looking at one particular (fixed) location – say, x,y,z – in the fluid, then ∂⁡f/∂⁡t gives the rate of change of f at that position.

On the other hand, we might want to know how f changes as we move along with the fluid. In this case, we might imagine attaching ourselves to a certain fluid element – a small ‘‘blob’’ of the fluid – and measuring how f changes as we move along with it. This rate of change is given by the so-called total or material derivative,22There are many other names for this kind of derivative, too; according to Wolfram MathWorld, it’s also called the convective derivative, the advective derivative, derivative following the motion, hydrodynamic derivative, and Lagrangian derivative, among a few others.

D⁢fD⁢t≡d⁢fd⁢t=dd⁢t⁢f⁢[x⁢(t),y⁢(t),z⁢(t),t].

Now, x⁢(t), y⁢(t), and z⁢(t) all change as we move with the flow, and of course

d⁢xd⁢t=u,d⁢yd⁢t=v,and d⁢zd⁢t=w.

So, applying the chain rule to the derivative above, we get

D⁢fD⁢t =∂⁡f∂⁡x⁢d⁢xd⁢t+∂⁡f∂⁡y⁢d⁢yd⁢t+∂⁡f∂⁡z⁢d⁢zd⁢t+∂⁡f∂⁡t
=u⁢∂⁡f∂⁡x+v⁢∂⁡f∂⁡y+w⁢∂⁡f∂⁡z+∂⁡f∂⁡t.

This can be written more compactly using vectors:

D⁢fD⁢t=∂⁡f∂⁡t+(𝐮⋅∇)f. (1.9)

Careful with that dot product; the gradient operator ∇ operates on the function f, so you can’t switch the order. In this case, (𝐮⋅∇)≠(∇⋅𝐮).

As an example of applying the total derivative, let’s take f to be the fluid velocity itself:

D⁢𝐮D⁢t=∂⁡𝐮∂⁡t+(𝐮⋅∇)𝐮. (1.10)

This, as you might suspect, is the acceleration of the fluid element at position 𝐱.

Note that if the material derivative is zero,

D⁢fD⁢t=0,

that implies that the quantity f doesn’t change for a particular fluid element. On the other hand, if just

(𝐮⋅∇)⁢f=0,

that means that f will be constant along a streamline. This isn’t obvious (at least to me), so let’s prove it. Let 𝐞^s be a unit vector that is always parallel to a streamline in the direction of the flow. Since a streamline is always tangent to the flow, we can write

𝐮=|𝐮|⁢𝐞^s,

so that

(𝐮⋅∇)⁢f=(|𝐮|⁢𝐞^s⋅∇)⁢f=(|𝐮|⁢∂∂⁡s)⁢f=|𝐮|⁢∂⁡f∂⁡s=0.

Thus ∂⁡f/∂⁡s=0 and the quantity f must be constant along the direction s, which is along the streamline.

  Example 1.5 – Acceleration of the fluid.

Let’s return to our flow around a stagnation point. What is the acceleration of the flow?

Well, clearly ∂⁡𝐮/∂⁡t=0 (the flow is steady), and so

D⁢𝐮D⁢t=(𝐮⋅∇)⁢𝐮=(u⁢∂∂⁡x+v⁢∂∂⁡y)⁢𝐮.

At this point is probably easiest to work out each vector component separately:

D⁢uD⁢t=u⁢∂⁡u∂⁡x+v⁢∂⁡u∂⁡y=α2⁢x,

and

D⁢vD⁢t=u⁢∂⁡v∂⁡x+v⁢∂⁡v∂⁡y=α2⁢y.

So the acceleration is

D⁢𝐮D⁢t=α2⁢x⁢i^+α2⁢y⁢j^=[α2⁢x,α2⁢y].
