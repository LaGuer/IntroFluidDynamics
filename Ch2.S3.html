2.3 Circular Flow

2.3.1 The Navier-Stokes Equations in Cylindrical Coordinates

The Navier-Stokes equations are trickier to write down in cylindrical coordinates, mainly due to the fact that the cylindrical unit vectors r^ and θ^ in Equation 1.3 are not constant – they depend on θ:

r^ =cos⁡θ⁢i^+sin⁡θ⁢j^
θ^ =-sin⁡θ⁢i^+cos⁡θ⁢j^.

When evaluating the derivatives in the Navier-Stokes equation, then, you have to be very careful to take this dependence into account. For example, note that

d⁢r^d⁢θ=θ^

and

d⁢θ^d⁢θ=-r^.

Furthermore, the gradient and Laplacian operators in cylindrical coordinates are a little more cumbersome than in Cartesian; I won’t bother writing them out here, but you should look them up on your own.88I really like the front and back covers in Griffiths’ Introduction to Electrodynamics for this. Altogether, here’s the Navier-Stokes equations in cylindrical coordinates for you:

∂⁡ur∂⁡t+(𝐮⋅∇)⁢ur-uθ2r =-1ρ⁢∂⁡p∂⁡r+ν⁢(∇2⁡ur-urr2-2r2⁢∂⁡uθ∂⁡θ)+gr (2.36)
∂⁡uθ∂⁡t+(𝐮⋅∇)⁢uθ+ur⁢uθr =-1ρ⁢r⁢∂⁡p∂⁡θ+ν⁢(∇2⁡uθ-uθr2+2r2⁢∂⁡ur∂⁡θ)+gθ (2.37)
∂⁡uz∂⁡t+(𝐮⋅∇)⁢uz =-1ρ⁢∂⁡p∂⁡z+ν⁢∇2⁡uz+gz (2.38)

where

(𝐮⋅∇) =ur⁢∂∂⁡r+uθr⁢∂∂⁡θ+uz⁢∂∂⁡z, (2.40)
∇2 =1r⁢∂∂⁡r⁢(r⁢∂∂⁡r)+1r2⁢∂2∂⁡θ2+∂2∂⁡z2. (2.41)

In addition, the incompressibility condition ∇⋅𝐮=0 becomes

1r⁢∂∂⁡r⁢(r⁢ur)+1r⁢∂⁡uθ∂⁡θ+∂⁡uz∂⁡z=0. (2.42)

2.3.2 Uniformly Rotating Fluid

For our first problem, we’ll consider fluid within a cylinder of radius a, as shown in Figure 3.4, with the cylinder rotating with an angular speed of Ω.

Already this is two dimensional flow – we’ll assume the cylinder is infinitely long, so there won’t be any z dependence of flow in that direction. But, to make things simpler, we also impose full cylindrical symmetry on our flow: the velocity will always take the form

𝐮=uθ⁢(r,t)⁢θ^. (2.43)

This means that the flow is always circular, with streamlines that are circles. Furthermore, the speed of the fluid only depends on the distance r from the origin, giving us the cylindrical symmetry. One nice benefit to a flow of this form is that the incompressibility condition is automatically satisfied, which you can check yourself with Problem 2.19.

As you might expect, taking this form of the velocity drastically reduces the Navier-Stokes equations. Equations 2.36 - 2.38 become

uθ2r =1ρ⁢∂⁡p∂⁡r (2.44)
∂⁡uθ∂⁡t =-1ρ⁢r⁢∂⁡p∂⁡θ+ν⁢(∂2⁡uθ∂⁡r2+1r⁢∂⁡uθ∂⁡r-uθr2) (2.45)
0 =-1ρ⁢∂⁡p∂⁡z. (2.46)

Note that I’ve completely dropped the gravity terms here – but see Problem 2 for the minimal effect that gravity has on our problems.

Now, the third equation above – the one for z – just says that the pressure p doesn’t depend on z at all, not surprising given the symmetry of the problem. The first equation gives us a relationship between the fluid velocity uθ and the radial pressure gradient, so if we know one we can get the other. The second equation is the important one, but we can clean it up a bit more.

Since uθ=uθ⁢(r,t) only, Equation 2.45 says that ∂⁡p/∂⁡θ must also be a function of only r and t. So we can write

∂⁡p∂⁡θ=P⁢(r,t),

where P⁢(r,t) is some unknown function. If we integrate this to find p, we get

p=P⁢(r,t)⁢θ+f⁢(r,t),

where f⁢(r,t) is the integration ‘‘constant’’ – remember, we have a partial derivative here (and note that there’s no z dependence thanks to Equation 2.46). But this shows us that the function P⁢(r,t) must be zero, since otherwise the pressure would be a multivalued function of position; it would take on different values for θ=0 and θ=2⁢π, for example, even though those two angles represent the same point in space.

Equation 2.45 then becomes, with P⁢(r,t)=∂⁡p/∂⁡θ=0,

∂⁡uθ∂⁡t=ν(∂2⁡uθ∂⁡r2+1r∂⁡uθ∂⁡r-uθr2). (2.47)

This equation determines the evolution of the fluid velocity.

Now we can go back to our original problem – fluid inside a rotating cylinder. We’ll first assume the fluid is steady, so that ∂⁡uθ/∂⁡t=0. Multiplying Equation 2.47 by r2 gives us

r2⁢d2⁢uθd⁢r2+r⁢d⁢uθd⁢r-uθ=0. (2.48)

Notice that the viscosity has dropped out of the problem. To solve this ordinary differential equation, let’s ‘‘guess’’ at a power-law solution of the form

uθ⁢(r)=rm.

Plugging this form into our equation leads to the quadratic

m2-1=0,

which has roots m=+1 and m=-1. Our general solution is then

uθ⁢(r)=A⁢r+Br. (2.49)

Since we have viscous fluid, one of our boundary conditions must be that the fluid moves with the same velocity as the cylinder wall, so that

uθ⁢(a)=Ω⁢a.

We don’t really have another boundary condition, since there isn’t another boundary; however, note that the second term in Equation 2.49 blows up at r=0, the centre of the cylinder. So, to keep the fluid velocity everywhere finite, we need to set B=0. The boundary condition then leads to A=Ω, so our solution is

uθ⁢(r)=Ω⁢r. (2.50)

This flow is in solid body rotation – in other words, the fluid rotates in exactly the same way as a solid body would, at constant angular speed Ω.

2.3.3 Spin Down of Rotating Fluid

What happens to the fluid if the rotating cylinder is suddenly brought to rest? Now we have time-dependent flow, and we’ll have to use the full Equation 2.47 to model what happens. Just a warning before we proceed: this is a pretty complicated problem to solve, and will require some knowledge about Bessel functions. We’ll take it slow, but it might help to ready up on them first.

We’ll use separation of variables to solve the partial differential equation, but first we should write out the initial and boundary conditions. The initial condition is of course our steady state solution we just found, Equation 2.50,

uθ(r,t=0)=Ωr. (2.51)

We’ll suppose the cylinder is brought to rest at t=0, so after that the no-slip condition says that

uθ⁢(a,t)=0 for ⁢t>0. (2.52)

As with our last problem, we’ll also have to make sure that the velocity is finite everywhere in the fluid.

Let

uθ⁢(r,t)=R⁢(r)⁢T⁢(t)

and plug that into Equation 2.47. Dividing both sides by R⁢T gives

1ν⁢T⁢d⁢Td⁢t=1R⁢d2⁢Rd⁢r2+1r⁢R⁢d⁢Rd⁢r-1r2.

The left hand side is a function of time t only, while the right hand side depends only on r. Since they’re independent variables, both sides must be equal to a constant, which, with the benefit of hindsight, we’ll call -k2.

The left hand side becomes

d⁢Td⁢t=-ν⁢k2⁢T,

which has the solution

T⁢(t)=C⁢e-ν⁢k2⁢t, (2.53)

where C is a constant. The right hand side is a little more difficult. With a little manipulation (multiplying both sides by r2⁢R) we can write it as

r2⁢d2⁢Rd⁢r2+r⁢d⁢Rd⁢r+(k2⁢r2-1)⁢R=0.

One more small change: let

x=k⁢r.

Then the equation becomes

x2⁢d2⁢Rd⁢x2+x⁢d⁢Rd⁢x+(x2-1)⁢R=0. (2.54)

This equation isn’t easy to solve by hand, but it turns out to be a fairly famous differential equation that pops up in various places in physics (like electromagnetism and acoustics) – it’s called Bessel’s equation, and the solutions are called Bessel functions.

The Bessel function of the first kind is usually written Jα⁢(x), where α is called the order of the Bessel function. In our case, with the (x2-1) term in the equation, we’re dealing only with the first order, so α=1. The second solution is the Bessel function of the second kind, sometimes called the Neumann function, Yα⁢(x). We can’t write either function in closed form – the best we can do is a series solution – but I’ve plotted both J1⁢(x) and Y1⁢(x) in Figure 2.10. Note that both functions are oscillatory, but not with a nice regular period like sine and cosine; Table 2.1 lists a few of the zeros of each, and we’ll need those in a moment.

The general solution to Equation 2.54 is the superposition of both Bessel functions,

R⁢(x)=A⁢J1⁢(x)+B⁢Y1⁢(x).

However, we can see from Figure 2.10 that the second function goes to negative infinity at the origin. This is no good – we want the velocity of the fluid to always be finite – so we can drop the this function and set B=0. Going back to using r instead of x, we have

R⁢(r)=A⁢J1⁢(k⁢r).

Now we can apply our boundary condition at r=a. I’ll write it out for you:

R⁢(a)=A⁢J1⁢(k⁢a)=0.

Clearly, we can’t set A=0, since that will give us a static fluid; instead, we’ll have to use the fact that the Bessel function has an infinite number of zeros, just like we did with the sine function back in Section 2.2.3. Unfortunately, the Bessel zeros aren’t at nicely periodic locations like n⁢π; as you can see in Table 2.1, they’re neither periodic nor at nice locations. Instead, we’ll have to be more general, so I’ll call the nth zero of the Bessel function λn. That is,

J1⁢(λn)=0

for every value of λn. The boundary condition thus implies that k⁢a=λn, or

k=λna.

Let’s put our solution so far together. We’ll combine the results for T⁢(t) and R⁢(r) to get the fluid velocity

uθ⁢(r,t)=R⁢(r)⁢T⁢(t)=A⁢J1⁢(λn⁢r/a)⁢e-λn2⁢ν⁢t/a2,

where I’ve combined the two constants into A. As is usual with separation of variables, we end up with not just one solution, but an infinite number of them. That means the most general solution is a linear combination of all of them,

uθ⁢(r,t)=∑n=1∞An⁢J1⁢(λn⁢r/a)⁢e-λn2⁢ν⁢t/a2. (2.55)

Even though we’ve been dealing with Bessel function, the process of separation of variables is the same as we went through in Section 2.2.2 – if you can follow that discussion, you should be okay here, too. We just have one more step: finding the values of the constants An that will match our initial conditions. This part is similar to before, too, but does require a bit of advanced knowledge of Bessel functions, so watch carefully.

Our initial conditions, from Equation 2.51 say

uθ⁢(r,0)=∑n=1∞An⁢J1⁢(λn⁢r/a)=Ω⁢r. (2.56)

Just like sinusoidal functions, Bessel functions are orthogonal, although it looks a little different; it turns out that99See, for example, Arfken and Weber’s Mathematical Methods for Physicists, Fourth Edition, p. 646.

∫0aJ1⁢(λn⁢r/a)⁢J1⁢(λm⁢r/a)⁢r⁢𝑑r=0

if m≠n. If m=n, then the result is

∫0aJ12⁢(λn⁢r/a)⁢r⁢𝑑r=a22⁢J22⁢(λn)

(notice that’s the second order Bessel function showing up on the right hand side).

We can use these results to solve for the Ans. Multiply both sides of Equation 2.56 by r⁢J1⁢(λm⁢r/a) and integrate from 0 to a:

∑n=1∞An⁢∫0aJ1⁢(λn⁢r/a)⁢J1⁢(λm⁢r/a)⁢r⁢𝑑r=Ω⁢∫0ar2⁢J1⁢(λm⁢r/a)⁢𝑑r.

Orthogonality allow us to collapse the sum – only the mth term survives – and, performing the integrations, we get

An=2⁢Ω⁢aλn⁢J2⁢(λn).

Putting this result together with our general solution gives us, finally,

uθ⁢(r,t)=2⁢Ω⁢a⁢∑n=1∞J1⁢(λn⁢r/a)λn⁢J2⁢(λn)⁢e-λn2⁢ν⁢t/a2. (2.57)

Unlike our steady state solution, this one depends on the viscosity of the fluid – it controls how long it takes for the fluid to slow down. I’ve plotted the velocity in Figure 2.11, where you can see how the fluid behaves over time, but it might be useful to take a more physical look at this problem. I have a water bottle on my desk right now, half-filled with water, with a radius of about 4 cm. The viscosity of water, from Table 1.1, is ν=0.01 cm2/s. Taking, as we did before, the first term only in the sum,

uθ⁢(r,t)≈2⁢Ω⁢a⁢J1⁢(λ1⁢r/a)λ1⁢J2⁢(λ1)⁢e-λ12⁢ν⁢t/a2.

It’s the exponential term we really want to look at; the characteristic time is

τ=a2ν⁢λ12≈110⁢ s.

That’s a long time for my water bottle to slow down once I’ve rotated up to some speed. However, when I eyeball it, it looks like it takes about 25 s to come to a complete stop – much quicker! The thing we’re missing in our analysis, of course, is the bottom of the bottle – it apparently plays an important role in the spin down of the water in the bottle.

2.3.4 The Line Vortex

Our last two examples involved fluid inside a cylinder; let’s flip that around and look at the fluid outside a solid cylinder, rotating initially at some angular speed Ω (see Figure 2.12). As we’ve done a number of times in this Chapter, we’ll search first for a steady-state solution, and then look at time-dependence.

We once again have circular flow, and the steady-state is described by Equation 2.48 and its general solution, Equation 2.49,

uθ⁢(r)=A⁢r+Br.

Although the boundary condition is also the same – that uθ⁢(a)=Ω⁢a – the difference here comes from keeping the fluid velocity finite. Since the fluid extends out to infinity, we set A=0; since there’s no fluid at r=0, the second term won’t be a problem. Fitting the boundary condition leads to our answer,

uθ⁢(r)=Ω⁢a2r.

We’ve seen this kind of flow before – it’s the line vortex from Example 1.5.2, with k=Ω⁢a2. In fact, it’s customary to write this flow as

uθ⁢(r)=Γ02⁢π⁢r, (2.58)

where the constant Γ0 is called the circulation. We’ll explore the concept of circulation more in Chapter LABEL:chap_complex_pot.

Now let’s take the cylinder away. But we’ll do it carefully – we’ll let a→0 at t=0, and the radius will shrink to zero instantaneously (kind of like how the boundary started moving in Section 2.2.3). That means Equation 2.58 will give us the initial conditions, but now the evolution of the system is governed by Equation 2.47,

∂⁡uθ∂⁡t=ν⁢(∂2⁡uθ∂⁡r2+1r⁢∂⁡uθ∂⁡r-uθr2).

Before we solve this, I want to make a small change to this differential equation. Let’s define a new variable,

Γ⁢(r,t)=2⁢π⁢r⁢uθ⁢(r,t). (2.59)

Then, with a bit of work, the evolution equation becomes slightly simpler,

∂⁡Γ∂⁡t=ν⁢(∂2⁡Γ∂⁡r2-1r⁢∂⁡Γ∂⁡r), (2.60)

and the initial conditions are now given by

Γ⁢(r,0)=Γ0. (2.61)

What about the boundary conditions? Well, as usual we want the flow to be finite everywhere for t>0 (careful – right at t=0 the flow uθ is in fact infinite, a point we’ll discuss in the next section). With our definition of Γ, that means we need

Γ⁢(0,t)=0 for ⁢t>0. (2.62)

Now we’re ready to solve this problem. Note that it’s actually scale-free – there’s nothing here (once the cylinder disappears) to provide any physical scale. That suggests we can seek a self-similar solution. As we did before in Section 2.2.4, we can define a new dimensionless variable,

η=rν⁢t, (2.63)

and assume that the solution is some function a η alone,

Γ⁢(r,t)=f⁢(η).

I’ll let you plug this into Equation 2.60 and find the similarity solution yourself (Problem 2.19). The answer is

Γ⁢(r,t)=Γ0⁢(1-e-r2/4⁢ν⁢t),

so that the fluid velocity is

uθ⁢(r,t)=Γ02⁢π⁢r⁢(1-e-r2/4⁢ν⁢t). (2.64)

Let’s explore this solution a bit. I’ve plotted the velocity in Figure 2.13 for a couple of different times so you can see how the speed decays. For large radii, r≫4⁢ν⁢t, we can drop the exponential term in Equation 2.64, and the behaviour far from the origin still looks like a line vortex:

uθ≈Γ02⁢π⁢r.

However, at small radii, r≪4⁢ν⁢t, we can expand the exponential and, keeping only the first two terms, get

uθ≈(Γ08⁢π⁢ν⁢t)⁢r.

This looks like solid body rotation again, with uθ∝r. Thus, at small radii, the flow is completely different; it’s undergoes solid body rotation and has vorticity. It’s the role of vorticity we need to discuss next.
