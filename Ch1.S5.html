1.5 Incompressible and Irrotational Flow

1.5.1 The Incompressibility Condition

Except in one notable case – gas dynamics – we’ll be dealing with fluids that are incompressible. This essentially just means that the density of the fluid is constant under pressure – you can’t compress it; stated another way, it means that a ‘‘dyed’’ blob of fluid can’t change in volume as it moves. Water is a good example of an incompressible fluid, and we’ll even treat air as incompressible in some cases.

We need to be able to write down a mathematical statement of incompressibility. To do this, consider a fixed, closed surface S in the fluid. By ‘‘fixed,’’ I mean that it’s fixed in space, so fluid can move into this closed volume V at some places and out at others; if the fluid is incompressible, though, then however much fluid is entering the region must equal the amount that is leaving.

Consider, then, an infinitesimally small piece of S – call it d⁢S (see Figure 1.4). The amount of fluid that is leaving V through d⁢S is given by

𝐮⋅n^⁢d⁢S.

This expression, where 𝐮 is the fluid velocity at the location of d⁢S and n^ points normal to the surface d⁢S but in the outward direction, is a volume flow rate. It measures the volume of fluid that passes through d⁢S per unit time. The dot product ensureS we get only that fluid which is moving through the tiny surface, whether it’s flowing outward (positive dot product) or inward (negative dot product).

To find the total amount of fluid leaving the entire surface S, we can just integrate over that surface:

∫S𝐮⋅n^⁢𝑑S

is the net volume rate at which fluid is leaving. However, for an incompressible fluid, we know this must be zero.

To write this in a form a little more usable, we can apply the divergence theorem (you remember that from vector calculus, right?),

∫S𝐅⋅n^⁢𝑑S=∫V∇⋅𝐅⁢𝑑V,

where 𝐅 is some general vector field. Our incompressibility condition then becomes

∫V∇⋅𝐮⁢𝑑V=0.

However, this must be true anywhere in the fluid and for any size volume; that means the integrand itself must be zero:

∇⋅𝐮=0. (1.11)

This is the incompressibility condition; unless we say otherwise, every flow must obey it.

  Example 1.6 – Incompressible flow.

Is our flow about a stagnation point incompressible? Let’s check. Recall that 𝐮=[α⁢x,-α⁢y]. The divergence of 𝐮 is then

∇⋅𝐮=∂⁡u∂⁡x+∂⁡v∂⁡y=α+(-α)=0,

just as we require.  

1.5.2 Vorticity

The vorticity of a flow is defined as

𝝎=∇×𝐮. (1.12)

For a two dimensional flow 𝐮=[u⁢(x,y),v⁢(x,y),0], we can write the vorticity as 𝝎=[0,0,ω], where

ω=∂⁡v∂⁡x-∂⁡u∂⁡y

(note the difference between the bolded 𝝎 and the non-bold ω – one is the full vector, and one is just the magnitude of that vector).

What, exactly, is vorticity? It’s related to the rotation of the flow, but is a little trickier than that. A couple of examples is probably best first.

  Example 1.7 – Vorticity of shear flow.

The flow given by 𝐮=[β⁢y,0,0] is called a shear flow; a vector plot is shown in Figure 1.5. This fluid is clearly not rotating, but it does have vorticity. Since it’s two dimensional, we can easily calculate ω:

ω=∂⁡v∂⁡x-∂⁡u∂⁡y=-β.

 

  Example 1.8 – Vorticity of line vortex.

Next, let’s look at the flow

𝐮=kr⁢θ^. (1.13)

This is written in cylindrical coordinates, and the vector plot is shown in Figure 1.6. We’ll be seeing this kind of flow quite a bit, so make sure you’re familiar with it – but for now all we need to know is that the fluid is clearly rotating. Despite this, the vorticity is actually zero; I’ll let you show this for homework (see Problem 1).  

Despite these examples, vorticity really is related to the rotation of the flow – but it’s the local rotation that it measures. If we built a ‘‘vorticity meter,’’ using two pieces of plastic and marking one tip so we can see it, the difference between a globally rotating flow and the line vortex example is very noticeable; see Figure 1.7.

Finally, a bit of terminology: if the vorticity of a flow is zero, we say that it is irrotational, so that

∇×𝐮=0 (1.14)

for an irrotational flow.
