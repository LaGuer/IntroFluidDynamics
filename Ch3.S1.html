3.1 Static Fluids

The simplest solution to either the Navier-Stokes equation or Euler’s equation is the trivial one: suppose the fluid is at rest, so that 𝐮=0 everywhere. Then Euler’s equation reduces to

0=-1ρ⁢∇⁡p+𝐠,

or

∇⁡p=ρ⁢𝐠.

If we take the direction of gravity to be down, so that 𝐠=[0,0,-g], this equation says

∂⁡p∂⁡x=0,∂⁡p∂⁡y=0,∂⁡p∂⁡z=-ρ⁢g.

The first two equations just say that the pressure p doesn’t depend explicitly on x or y, and we can integrate the third to get

p=p0-ρ⁢g⁢z, (3.3)

where p0 is the integration constant. If our fluid has a ‘‘free surface’’ – it’s open to the atmosphere – at z=0, then p0 is the atmospheric pressure.

This result is simple and tells us what we pretty much know: as you go down in depth (z<0) in a fluid, the pressure increases. Note, however, that we’ve assumed the density ρ stays constant; in some fluids (like the atmosphere) it’s a bad assumption, while in others (like the ocean, at least for reasonable depths) it’s okay.
