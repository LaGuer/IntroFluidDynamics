3.3 The Vorticity Equation

Let’s rewrite Euler’s equation again, this time in terms of the vorticity. Going back to equation (3.6) and writing 𝝎=∇×𝐮, we have

∂⁡𝐮∂⁡t+𝝎×𝐮=-∇⁡H.

Take the curl of both sides:

∂⁡𝝎∂⁡t+∇×(𝝎×𝐮)=-∇×∇⁡H.

But the curl of a gradient is identically zero (see Problem 1), so the right hand side vanishes.

Now, there’s a vector identity (another one!) that says, for two vectors 𝐅 and 𝐆, that

∇×(𝐅×𝐆)=(𝐆⋅∇)⁢𝐅-(𝐅⋅∇)⁢𝐆+𝐅⁢(∇⋅𝐆)-𝐆⁢(∇⋅𝐅) (3.9)

(see Problem 3). Using this in the equation above, with 𝐅→𝝎 and 𝐆→𝐮, gives us

∂⁡𝝎∂⁡t+(𝐮⋅∇)⁢𝝎-(𝝎⋅∇)⁢𝐮+𝝎⁢(∇⋅𝐮)-𝐮⁢(∇⋅𝝎)=0.

Of course, we’re dealing with an ideal fluid, so ∇⋅𝐮=0, and, since 𝝎 is a curl, ∇⋅𝝎=0 identically (see Problem 1 again for that one). So the fourth and fifth terms vanish, and we have

∂⁡𝝎∂⁡t+(𝐮⋅∇)⁢𝝎=(𝝎⋅∇)⁢𝐮.

Finally, we can combine the two terms on the right hand side – that’s the definition of the material derivative of 𝝎 – and we have, at long last, the vorticity equation,

D⁢𝝎D⁢t=(𝝎⋅∇)𝐮. (3.10)

This equation will prove useful every once in a while for us. In particular, for a two dimensional flow, where

𝝎=(0,0,ω),

it’s easy to see that the right hand side becomes zero, and

D⁢𝝎D⁢t=0. (3.11)

This means that the vorticity of each individual fluid element is conserved, a result we’ll use later. Furthermore, if the flow is also steady, this becomes

(𝐮⋅∇)⁢ω=0, (3.12)

and the vorticity is constant along streamlines in this case.
