3.2 Bernoulli’s Principle

For our next result, we’ll first need to rewrite Euler’s equation. Since gravity is a conservative force, we can always write it in terms of the gradient of a scalar, so that

𝐠=-∇⁡χ,

where χ is the gravitational potential. For example, setting χ=g⁢z gives us our usual 𝐠 pointing down along the z axis.

With this change, Euler’s equation becomes

∂⁡𝐮∂⁡t+(𝐮⋅∇)⁢𝐮=-∇⁡(pρ+χ).

It turns out that the second term on the left can be written as (see Problem 3)

(𝐮⋅∇)⁢𝐮=(∇×𝐮)×𝐮+∇⁡(12⁢𝐮2). (3.4)

Note that I’ve written 𝐮2 for 𝐮⋅𝐮; as usual, be careful of all the different u’s we deal with. With this substitution, and moving the gradient onto the right hand side, Euler’s equation is now

∂⁡𝐮∂⁡t+(∇×𝐮)×𝐮=-∇⁡(pρ+12⁢𝐮2+χ). (3.5)

This doesn’t look any better than the original form of Euler’s equation, though. We can clean it up a bit by defining

H≡pρ+12⁢𝐮2+χ,

which is sometimes called the ‘‘total head’’ or ‘‘energy head’’ of the flow. If we also assume the flow is steady, we then have

(∇×𝐮)×𝐮=-∇⁡H. (3.6)

One last step – take the dot product with 𝐮 for both sides:

𝐮⋅[(∇×𝐮)×𝐮]=-𝐮⋅∇⁡H.

But now the left hand side is zero – the term in the square brackets has a direction perpendicular to 𝐮, so the dot product with 𝐮 vanishes.

So we’re left, finally, with Bernoulli’s streamline theorem, which says that, for steady flow,

(𝐮⋅∇)H=0. (3.7)

This means that H is constant along a streamline for steady flow.

If the fluid is also irrotational, so that ∇×𝐮=0, equation (3.6) gives us the stronger statement

∇H=0, (3.8)

so that H is constant everywhere in the fluid.

  Example 3.1 – A leaky bucket.

A large tank of water, open to the atmosphere at the top, suddenly springs a leak near the bottom (see Figure 3.1). If the hole is 1.0 m below the free surface, what is the speed of the water as it comes out the hole?

This is a good case for Bernoulli’s principle, since the flow is (approximately) steady – the surface will drop only slowly if the hole is small. We can therefore use the streamline theorem:

H=pρ+12⁢𝐮2+g⁢z=constant.

Presumably there exists a streamline that connects the free surface at the top of the tank with the hole (joining points 1 and 2 as shown in Figure 3.1). Then we can evaluate H at both points:

  • •

    Point 1 →H1=p1ρ+12⁢𝐮12+g⁢z1

  • •

    Point 2 →H2=p2ρ+12⁢𝐮22+g⁢z2

But p1=p2=p0 since both locations are open to the atmosphere. We’ll also take 𝐮1=0, since the surface drops only very slowly. Finally, using the coordinate system shown in Figure 3.1, we have z2=0 (and z1=1 m).

Setting H1=H2 then gives us

p0ρ+g⁢z1=p0ρ+12⁢𝐮22.

The pressure terms cancel, and we can rearrange for the speed of the water:

|𝐮|=2⁢g⁢z1≈4.4⁢ m/s.
